EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Project: "Volatility Forecasting, Regime Dynamics, and Risk Modeling in Chinese Equity Markets"

1. RESEARCH OBJECTIVE
This project translates complex mathematical risk modeling into actionable portfolio strategy. I developed a financial econometrics framework specifically designed to analyze twenty years of high-volatility structural dynamics within the Chinese equity indices (CSI 300, SSE Composite, ChiNext). The platform evaluates predictive machine learning and GARCH forecasting accuracy. It dynamically links these models directly to portfolio exposure. The objective is to demonstrate how predictive models perform when applied to dynamic capital preservation during unprecedented structural shifts.

2. KEY FINDINGS
The Chinese equity landscape exhibits distinct structural characteristics driven by significant retail participation and state-directed policy interventions. These factors create an environment defined by persistent, clustered "regimes" rather than random noise. While Machine Learning architectures effectively captured variance during stable trends, their performance diminished during crisis events (e.g., the 2015 margin constraints). Without an inherent autoregressive structure, feature-based models struggled to extrapolate unprecedented shocks. Consequently, standard static strategies consistently experienced severe drawdowns. Furthermore, this research proved that simple Value-at-Risk modeling systematically underestimates crash momentum in China; when risk boundaries are breached, the failures demonstrate significant serial dependence.

3. ECONOMIC SIGNIFICANCE
Statistical accuracy lacks practical utility without economic execution. The framework concludes that by employing robust GARCH modeling to dynamically adjust portfolio exposure as forecasted variance increases (Inverse Volatility Targeting), a strategy can substantially reduce its Maximum Drawdown. By systematically reducing capital allocation directly ahead of a Markov-modeled volatility spike, the overall risk-adjusted performance (measured by the Sharpe Ratio) increases significantly. This approach effectively bridges the theoretical gap between complex econometrics and institutional portfolio survival.
